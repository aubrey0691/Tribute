			<hr style="width:350px" >
			<p>Florence Nightingale (1820-1910), known as “The Lady With the Lamp,” was a British nurse, social reformer and statistician best known as the founder of modern nursing. Her experiences as a nurse during the Crimean War were foundational in her views about sanitation. She established St. Thomas’ Hospital and the Nightingale Training School for Nurses in 1860. Her efforts to reform healthcare greatly influenced the quality of care in the 19 and 20 centuries.

		<div class="w3-padding-64 w3-content" id="timeline">
			<h2 class="w3-text-black">Florence Nightingale's Life Timeline:</h2>
			<hr style="width:350px">
				<div id="tribute-info">
					<h3>12 May 1820</h3>
					<p>Born in Florence, Italy.</p>
					<h3>7 February 1837</h3>
					<p>Dreamt that God told her she had a great mission in life, which led her to think about becoming a nurse.</p>
					<h3>1844</h3>
					<p>Announced her decision to take up nursing.</p>
					<h3>1851</h3>
					<p>Studied nursing at the Institute of Protestant Deaconesses in Kaiserwerth, Germany.</p>
					<h3>22 August 1853</h3>
					<p>Became superintendent at the Institute for the Care of Sick Gentlewomen, in London.</p>
					<h3>1853-1856</h3>
					<p>The Crimean War: Russia v. UK, France, Sardinia and the Ottomon Empire about owning territories in the Ottomon Empire.</p>
					<h3>21 October 1854</h3>
					<p>Left for the main British camp in the Crimea, along with 38 volunteer nurses trained by Florence.</p>
					<h3>4 November 1854</h3>
					<p>Arrived at the Selimiye Barracks in Scutari in Turkey.</p>
					<h3>May 1855</h3>
					<p>Became very ill with Crimean fever, a chronic disease.</p>
					<h3>16 March 1856</h3>
					<p>Became general superintendent of the Female Nursing Establishment of the Military Hospitals of the Army.</p>
					<h3>August 1956</h3>
					<p>Returned to England from Crimea.</p>
					<h3>September 1856</h3>
					<p>Visited Queen Victoria and Prince Albert to tell them about the poor conditions of military hospitals and asked that a Royal Commission investigate the health of the British Army.</p>
					<h3>1858</h3>
					<p>Published Notes on Matters affecting the Health, Efficiency and Hospital Administration of the British Army.</p>
					<h3>1858-59</h3>
					<p>Worked to establish a Royal Commission to investigate health conditions in India.</p>
					<h3>1859</h3>
					<p>Published Suggestions for Thought to Searchers after Religious Truths. <br>
					Published Notes on Nursing: What it is and What it is Not. <br>
					Became a member of the Royal Statistical Society.</p>
					<h3>9 July 1860</h3>
					<p>Opened the Nightingale Training School at St. Thomas' Hospital.</p>
					<h3>1861</h3>
					<p>Opened the School of Midwifery Nursing at King’s College Hospital.</p>
					<h3>1868</h3>
					<p>Opening of The East London Nursing Society.</p>
					<h3>1874</h3>
					<p>Opening of the Workhouse Nursing Association and National Society for Providing Trained Nurses for the Poor.</p>
					<h3>1883</h3>
					<p>Awarded the Royal Red Cross by Queen Victoria.</p>
					<h3>1890</h3>
					<p>Opening of the Queen's Jubilee Nursing Institute.</p>
					<h3>1901</h3>
					<p>Became completely blind.</p>
					<h3>1904</h3>
					<p>Appointed a Lady of Grace of the Order of St John.</p>
					<h3>1907</h3>
					<p>Awarded the Order of Merit.</p>
					<h3>1908</h3>
					<p>Given the Honorary Freedom of the City of London.</p>
					<h3>1910</h3>
					<p>Awarded the Badge of Honour by the Norwegian Red Cross Society.</p>
					<h3>13 August 1910</h3>
					<p>Died in London, UK.</p>
